## Full Draft — Post 3: The Morning Check-In Protocol

*Rationale: No wellness tip has been drafted across three weeks of planning. This is the pillar most aligned with the core audience (fitness enthusiasts, HR leaders) and where Shah's NESTA certification and competition background provide a genuine credibility moat. High save and share potential.*

---

I used to start every workday by opening my inbox.

Competition prep cured me of that.

When you're 10 weeks out from a national bodybuilding stage, you cannot afford to make decisions by feel.

Every training session, every meal, every recovery window has to earn its place in the plan.

So you learn to check the right inputs before you do anything else.

Before email. Before Slack. Before the calendar.

Four numbers:

**1. Bodyweight trend (7-day rolling average)**
A single morning weigh-in means nothing. A directional pattern tells you whether your system is working. The same logic applies to content metrics — individual post performance lies; weekly trends tell the truth.

**2. Sleep quality score**
Not hours. Quality. Deep sleep and REM cycles determine whether your brain has actually recovered or is just technically awake. A night below threshold is not a day to make big strategic calls.

**3. Resting heart rate**
A reliable proxy for nervous system recovery. Elevated RHR after hard training means the body isn't finished. Elevated RHR before a high-stakes meeting means your system is already in overdrive before the room fills. Knowing the difference changes how you show up.

**4. Subjective readiness (1–10)**
How do you feel? Not as a vibe — as a tracked data point. Over time, your gut readiness score correlates directly with cognitive output. Learn the pattern and you learn how hard to push on any given day.

The whole check-in takes 90 seconds.

Elite athletes don't guess training intensity. They measure, then adjust.

Knowledge workers show up at "full capacity" every single day — or perform like they do — and wonder why they're burning out without compounding progress.

The protocol isn't complicated.

But the discipline to do it before the inbox opens? That's the actual practice.
