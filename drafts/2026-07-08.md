### Idea 1 — Professional Insight: What I Look For When Hiring for AI Marketing Roles ← DRAFTED BELOW

**Title:** "The criteria I used to hire marketers in 2023 are mostly wrong for 2026. Here's what's changed — and what I look for now."

**Pillar:** AI in Marketing / Career & Leadership

**Angle:** The Week 35 Professional Insight (publish July 29) describes how a working AI marketing workflow is structured — three stages, the feedback loop, defined handoffs. Week 36 Professional Insight is the natural operational follow-through: who do you need on the team to run that system? Shah's specific title (Manager of AI Marketing) and his track record of hiring and developing the team that built the Vantage Circle AI content operation gives him genuine, non-generic authority here.

The post is not a list of AI tool certifications or a call for "prompt engineering" experience. It is three specific criteria that have emerged from building a real AI marketing workflow — criteria that differ meaningfully from what a pre-AI marketing hiring process would have valued:

1. **Judgment visibility** — Not "uses AI responsibly," but: can the candidate articulate the last thing they refused to let AI do and why? The skill is not tool literacy; it is knowing where the human check is required and being able to explain the reasoning. This is concrete and testable in an interview.

2. **Pattern fluency in data** — Distinct from "data-driven." The feedback loop in Stage 3 of the AI workflow (performance data flowing back into brief strategy) only works if someone can watch a signal change over time and form a hypothesis about why — without being told what to look for. This is different from reading a dashboard. It is a specific analytical instinct that most hiring processes don't screen for.

3. **Institutional documentation reflex** — The teams whose AI workflows improve over time are the ones that systematically document what broke, not just what worked. When an AI output failed the edit pass, why? When the brief produced a bad draft, what was the specific gap? A hire who builds failure documentation is more valuable than a hire who produces success they can't explain — because documented failure is what makes the workflow more precise next cycle.

Closes with the observation that these criteria reflect a genuine shift in what the job is: from "use these tools to produce this output" (2023) to "design and improve the system that produces output at scale, and know where the system fails" (2026). Different roles reward different things.

**Hook:** "The criteria I used to hire marketers in 2023 are mostly wrong for 2026."

**Why now:** August is H2 hiring season — marketing teams that made AI investment decisions in H1 are now staffing for H2 execution. The Week 35 post (July 29) closes with the workflow question: "What does your AI marketing workflow look like?" This post arrives one week later with the follow-up most managers are implicitly asking: "And who do you need on the team to actually run it?" The Week 35–36 arc is a complete operational conversation: here is the system, here is who you need to run it. Wednesday August 5 publish date.

### Idea 2 — Personal Story: What Being Directed as an Actor Taught Me About Managing a Team

**Title:** "I spent time as an actor. The best management lesson I ever got came from a director's note — not a business book."

**Pillar:** Career & Leadership / Personal Brand

**Angle:** Shah's acting background was first deployed in the Week 34 Wellness post (Stage Fright / performance psychology, publish July 24). This is its first appearance in the Personal Story pillar, which means a different and complementary angle is needed — not performance psychology under pressure, but the relational dynamics of the actor-director collaboration as a lens on leadership and team management.

Three specific transfers from Shah's acting experience to his management practice:

1. **Absorption without literalism** — A director's note arrives as a precise, character-specific instruction ("say this line with less certainty"). The actor's job is to find the internal truth that produces that adjustment — not to mechanically imitate the instruction. Shah describes how this trained his ability to receive strategic direction from leadership: the goal is to understand the intent and translate it into team-specific action, not to execute the literal command. Teams that can do this are self-directing; teams that can't require constant supervision.

2. **Ensemble discipline over solo performance** — Stage work taught Shah that a technically brilliant individual performance that doesn't serve the scene fails the production. The instinct to elevate the team rather than stand out individually — because the story only works when every character is coherent — was a specific discipline Shah carried into his management approach. The most visible person in the room is not always doing the most useful work.

3. **The notes culture** — In theatre, the director's post-rehearsal note is not praise or criticism. It is a specific adjustment for the next run. Shah adopted this framing for marketing retrospectives: a post-campaign debrief is not a judgment on the team's quality — it is a refinement of the next performance. The frame changes how people receive the feedback. Teams that are used to receiving notes (specific, non-personal, forward-looking) iterate faster than teams that receive only evaluations.

Closes with the observation that Shah's creative dimensions — acting, music, bodybuilding — are not separate from the professional identity. Each one is training in a specific mode of performance that has a direct analogue in how a team operates.

**Hook:** "I spent time as an actor. The best management framework I use today came from watching how directors give notes — not from a leadership book."

**Why now:** Week 36 Personal Story slot (Thursday August 6). The acting background had its first content deployment in the Week 34 Wellness post — this post arrives 13 days later with a completely different angle on the same credential, showing range rather than repetition. The Week 35 Personal Story (PMI-ACP, July 30) explored structured methodology as operational insight; this post arrives one week later as a contrasting, more personal and creative origin story. Strong resonance with HR/People leaders who are thinking about feedback culture and team communication; with managers who have never thought about theatre as a management school; with professionals in the Shah audience who have followed the building-from-Guwahati narrative and are curious about the creative identity dimensions. ### Idea 3 — Wellness Tip: Train Like an Athlete, Schedule Like One

**Title:** "I competed at the Mr. India level. The planning structure that made it possible has nothing to do with working out. Here's how I apply the same model to manage my professional year."

**Pillar:** Wellness & Discipline

**Angle:** The Weeks 31–35 wellness arc closed with sleep as the foundational variable tying together nutrition, recovery, music/cognition, performance psychology, and rest. Week 36 opens a new arc. This first post of the new arc deploys Shah's ACTION CPT credential and bodybuilding competition background in a mode not yet used: periodization — the structured training science of planned adaptation cycles — as a framework for professional performance and team scheduling.

The insight is not generic "rest is important." It is a specific structural argument: high-performance athletes don't train by applying consistent pressure until they crash. They plan loading phases, deload weeks, and progressive overload cycles deliberately, because adaptation requires both stimulus and recovery — and the ratio between them is where performance lives. Most knowledge workers apply only consistent pressure, take unplanned recovery when they crash, and don't understand why performance degrades despite sustained effort.

Three mechanisms from Shah's competition prep directly applicable to professional scheduling:

1. **Loading phase = concentrated focus periods** — A hypertrophy phase in training (high volume, high intensity, structured to cause productive stress) mirrors a major campaign or project sprint. The loading phase works because it is bounded — the athlete knows recovery is coming, so they can push harder during the load without protecting against an indefinite demand. Professional work that has no planned end date generates protective reserve, not maximal output.

2. **Deload week = deliberate strategic review** — After a heavy training cycle, the athlete reduces volume and lets adaptations consolidate. The equivalent in knowledge work is a structured week following a major campaign where output volume is deliberately reduced, reflection is the primary work, and the lessons from the loading phase are documented and integrated. This is not laziness — it is where performance gains become permanent instead of evaporating in the next sprint.

3. **Progressive overload = never running the same quarter twice** — The athlete who performs the same workout indefinitely adapts once and then plateaus. The professional who applies exactly the same approach each quarter plateaus the same way. Progressive overload means adding a specific new variable each cycle — a new channel, a new team structure, a new measurement layer — so the system is always at the edge of its current capacity, and adaptation continues.

Closes with the practical invitation: if you plan your workouts with more structural discipline than you plan your quarters — with loading phases, deload periods, and progressive overload built in — you already understand the model. The question is whether you're applying it.

**Hook:** "Athletes plan fatigue and recovery deliberately. Knowledge workers apply consistent pressure until they crash and call it burnout. The model for fixing this has been in sports science for decades."

**Why now:** Opens the new Weeks 36+ wellness arc after the five-week Weeks 31–35 arc closed with sleep. The new arc moves from individual performance variables (nutrition, recovery, sleep) to structural and systemic frameworks for performance management — a natural progression from "what to optimise" to "how to structure the optimisation." August timing is strong: professionals returning from any July breaks and re-entering execution mode; Q3 planning cycles in full swing; the "how do I structure the next quarter" question is live. The periodization framework from Shah's competition background gives this post authority that a standard "plan your quarters better" post cannot have. Publish Friday August 7.

## Full Draft — Professional Insight: Hiring for AI Marketing Roles

Shah's title (Manager of AI Marketing) and his track record of building the team that built the workflow give him direct standing to speak to this — not as prediction, but as practice. ---

The criteria I used to hire marketers in 2023 are mostly wrong for 2026.

Not slightly off. Wrong in ways that matter for how an AI marketing team actually runs.

Here's what's changed — and what I look for now.

---

**What I've stopped treating as a primary signal:**

Tool familiarity.

"Experience with [AI tool]" in a CV tells me almost nothing useful.

The tool landscape shifts every quarter. Someone who mastered the right tool 18 months ago may have optimised for a workflow that no longer exists.

I care less about which tools a candidate has used and more about how they made decisions about what to use — and, more specifically, what not to delegate.

---

**What I look for instead:**

**1. Judgment visibility**

Not "I believe in human oversight." A specific case.

Can the candidate articulate the last thing they refused to let AI do — and why?

This question reveals whether someone has actually been in the position of evaluating AI output against a real standard, or whether they've been in the position of using AI output and calling it done.

The second person may be fast. The first one is the person I can trust to run Stage 2 of the workflow — the human edit pass that covers what the AI cannot know: product context, audience nuance, judgment calls about whether a piece is ready to publish.

That person is harder to find than their CV suggests.

**2. Pattern fluency in data**

Not "data-driven." Everyone is data-driven.

I mean: can they watch a performance signal change over time and form a hypothesis about why — without being told what to look for?

The workflow we run at Vantage Circle has a feedback loop. Performance data flows back from distribution into brief strategy. The value of that loop depends entirely on someone being able to read what the data is flagging and update the brief accordingly.

Reading a dashboard is not the same skill as forming a hypothesis from a trend.

One of them is a reporting function. The other is what makes Stage 3 of the workflow actually compound over time.

**3. Institutional documentation reflex**

The teams whose AI workflows improve over time are the ones that systematically document what broke.

Not just what worked.

When an AI draft failed the edit pass — what was the specific gap? When a brief produced weak output — what was the misalignment? When the feedback loop produced a noisy signal — what skewed the data?

A hire who documents failure is worth more to the team than a hire who produces success they can't explain — because documented failure is what makes the next cycle of the workflow more precise.

Undocumented success is luck that can't be repeated. Documented failure is a system that's learning.

---

These three criteria reflect a genuine shift in what the role actually is.

In 2023, the job was: use these tools to produce this output.

In 2026, the job is: design and improve the system that produces output at scale — and know where the system fails.

Different roles. Different signals.

---

If you're hiring for AI marketing functions right now: what has changed in what you screen for?

And if you're building toward one of these roles: which of the three would you back as your strongest credential?
