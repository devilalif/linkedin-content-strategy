### Idea 3 — Professional Insight (AI Citation Tracking in Practice)

**Title:** "Everyone on our team is watching rankings and clicks. We started tracking something else in Q3. And it's already showing us where we're losing."

**Pillar:** AI in Marketing / SEO & Content Strategy

**Angle:** The Q2 close post (June 28) named brand mention velocity in AI-generated responses as a Q3 strategic bet. This post is the operational follow-up: what does it actually look like to track it? Three concrete methods: (1) weekly prompt audits — running the top 10 category questions your buyers would ask an LLM and logging whether your brand appears, in what position, and with what framing; (2) third-party citation gap analysis — comparing which competitors appear in AI answers versus which rank on traditional SERP for the same query cluster; (3) the "attribution gap" — the ratio between AI-driven sessions (users who arrive already knowing the brand) versus discovery sessions. The key insight: the gap between what ranks on page one and what gets cited by AI is not random — it maps closely to topical authority depth, not domain authority. You can have 3,000 backlinks and still not get cited because you've covered a topic broadly rather than deeply. Q3 strategy implication: depth of coverage on a narrow cluster beats breadth coverage across a wide one, every time.

**Hook:** "Our organic rankings held in Q2. But something else dropped — and we almost missed it entirely."

**Why now:** Natural operational follow-up to the Q2 close strategic post published this week. Readers who saved the Q2 close post were almost certainly thinking "okay, but how do you actually track AI citations?" — this post answers exactly that. First Monday of Q3, when marketing teams are making tool and measurement decisions for the quarter, is the highest-leverage timing for a post about what to measure.

The first 72 hours of H2 will set the frame for the next six months.

I'm not spending them on Q3 OKRs.

---

In competition prep, the week after a deload is not a return to last cycle's peak.

It's a recalibration.

You reset your training maxes from a recovered baseline — not from where you exhausted out. The intent is to compound from a reset, not just resume from a pause.

The first 72 hours of H2 are the professional equivalent of that reset session.

---

Most people spend them catching up.

The Q2 reports that didn't get filed. The campaigns that almost landed but didn't. The conversations that got deferred because June was too full.

I've done this. It feels productive.

It is not.

Catch-up loads a new sprint onto a system that hasn't had time to recalibrate. You carry Q2's unfinished weight into Q3 — and Q3's first month becomes Q2's extended tail instead of a fresh block.

---

What the first 72 hours of H2 are actually for — three commitments:

**1. How you'll protect deep work.**

Not a calendar block. A behavioral decision: what will you not let meetings do to your most productive four hours this quarter?

Blocks get moved. Commitments get defended.

If you can't name the specific thing you'll say no to in order to protect it, the time will fill anyway.

**2. How you'll signal priorities to your team.**

Not an OKR deck. One honest answer to: what is the one thing this team should be deciding more independently in Q3?

Most Q3 execution failures trace back to a brief that was understood differently by the person who wrote it and the team who executed it. Setting the frame early — explicitly, specifically — is the highest-leverage conversation you can have this week.

**3. How you'll track progress without maintaining a system.**

Most tracking setups become a second job by October.

In competition prep, my weekly check-in is three numbers: bodyweight, training weight, energy on a scale of 1–10. That's it. Everything else is noise until one of the three shifts.

What are your three numbers for Q3?

---

None of these take long.

All of them take longer to get right than you expect — because the version that actually works is specific to you, not borrowed from a productivity framework.

---

Q3 starts tomorrow.

The teams that compound into Q4 won't be the ones who hit it hardest on Monday morning.

They'll be the ones who spent Sunday setting three commitments they can actually keep — and Tuesday finding out which ones they already need to adjust.

---
