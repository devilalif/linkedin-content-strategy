This post is the operational follow-up: what does tracking it actually look like? Three concrete methods: (1) weekly prompt audits — running the top 10 category questions buyers would ask an LLM and logging whether the brand appears, in what position, and with what framing; (2) citation gap analysis — comparing which competitors appear in AI-generated answers versus which rank on traditional SERP for the same query cluster; (3) the attribution gap — the ratio between AI-driven sessions (users who arrive already knowing the brand) versus organic discovery sessions. I've done some acting work.

It's not in my LinkedIn headline. I rarely bring it up.

But it changed how I run difficult conversations with my team — and I only noticed because I stopped filling silences.

---

Acting training teaches things no leadership programme will.

Not because they're secrets. Because you can only absorb them inside a room, with a camera, watching yourself on a screen.

**Your internal state broadcasts — whether you intend it to or not.**

The camera doesn't see what you think you're showing.

It sees the bracing before a hard conversation. The "I already know how this will go" energy you carried into the room before anyone spoke. The quiet that settles differently when you've already mentally left the outcome.

None of that is visible to you. All of it is visible to the room.

I check what I'm actually carrying into a difficult conversation now — not just what I plan to say.

**Presence and confidence are not the same thing.**

Confidence, performed, is being unmoved.

Presence is the ability to be genuinely affected by what's happening in the room — while staying committed to the structure you brought in.

One closes conversations. The other opens them.

Most leadership training optimises for confidence. Acting training builds presence. The distinction matters more than it sounds.

**Silence is part of the communication.**

The pause before answering a hard question.

The moment after feedback lands — before the other person has fully processed it.

The gap between a challenge and your response.

Most people fill those gaps immediately. It feels like lost ground. It isn't.

That's where the other person decides whether you're actually listening.

---

The skills that make you visible in one context tend to make you more present in all of them.

The camera was the harshest feedback loop I've been in.

I recommend something like it — any practice where you have to watch yourself — for anyone who manages people.
