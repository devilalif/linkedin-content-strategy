I'm a certified sports nutrition specialist.

Most of what I know about peak cognitive performance I learned from competition prep.

---

Not from a productivity book.

Not from a morning routine thread.

From figuring out what I needed to eat — and when — to lift heavy, stay sharp, and recover fast enough to do it again the next day.

The knowledge-worker version of that problem is not as different as it sounds.

---

Three principles that cross over directly:

**1. Timing matters more than total.**

In competition prep, we don't just count macros for the day — we time them around the work that matters most.

Carbohydrate availability in the 60–90 minutes before a training session determines the quality of that session. Not energy in general. Specific energy, in a specific window.

The same mechanism operates cognitively.

What you eat in the 90 minutes before your most demanding mental work — a complex analysis, a difficult conversation, a strategic presentation — affects the quality of your thinking in ways most professionals attribute to mood or stress.

It's often metabolic. And it's correctable.

**2. Protein distribution is a clarity lever, not just a muscle one.**

The standard advice is to hit a daily protein target. That's incomplete.

Distributing the same total across four or five intake events throughout the day maintains the availability of dopamine and norepinephrine precursors across the full working window. That's not gym science — that's neuroscience.

If your breakfast is carb-heavy and protein-light, the cognitive flatness you feel by 11am has a nutritional explanation.

It is not willpower. It is substrate.

**3. The recovery window is real for the brain, not just the muscle.**

After a hard training session, there is a 45–60 minute window where glycogen replenishment is maximally efficient. Miss it, and recovery is measurably slower.

The cognitive equivalent exists.

After a high-stakes meeting, a difficult call, or three hours of deep work, you are in a metabolic trough. A protein-and-fast-carbohydrate intake in that window accelerates the return to baseline — and determines the quality of the next cognitive block, not just how you feel by evening.

Most professionals respond to that trough with more coffee.

---

None of this is a diet protocol.

This is performance architecture.

The bodybuilders who worked it out did so because competition left no margin for error in how they felt under pressure.

You do not need a competition stage to apply the same logic.
